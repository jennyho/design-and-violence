Happylife (Auger, Loizeau, Zwiggelaar, and Rajoub)

By Jenny Ho

From the designers: What would it mean for an electronic device to know more about your partner's emotional state than you do? Or be capable of predicting a future bout of misery through statistical analysis of accumulated data? When does technology become too invasive? Happylife explores these issues through the development of emergent real-time dynamic passive profiling techniques applied to mediate and display human emotive states in a family home.

The most shocking aspect about this product is that at some point in the future, our bodies no longer belong to us. Our bodies, personal thoughts, and emotions are no longer ours, but instead are made public and visible. We cannot hide our emotions from this device, so we can’t control what personal information is shown. In this situation, we lose the power to decide when to reveal information about ourselves within our homes.

The context of the home is disturbing, because home is no longer a safe place with Happylife. Ideally, we expect to have personal agency, privacy, and safety when we are at home. Auger and Loizeau give us a critical social question: what are the trade-offs we are will willing to make when it comes to safety? When we no longer feel safe at home, we are willing to give up personal agency and privacy. Happylife is the logical exaggeration of this trade-off: it brings techniques used to monitor crime and terror into a domestic space. As Auger and Loizeau note, the thermal imaging technology in Happylife already exists as measures for border control and for national security. Governments can noninvasively detect potential dangers. However, the accuracy of “noninvasive” as an adjective is questionable.

Within the past decade alone, many acts of violence occurred in public spaces that were assumed to be safe, and it’s not unreasonable to suspect that it will continue to happen. Escalating the use of advanced surveillance technology seems to be the appropriate response. Yet, intense profiling enables a quiet form of violence, because we are invading other people’s spaces and bodies to control them.

That’s the paradox of designing for safety. Sometimes we incite violence to prevent violence.

Aggravated by international and domestic attacks, our culture is that of fear. How do we determine who or what should be considered dangerous? When we figure out those characteristics, what happens when an innocent person is accused of being a risk? What are the limits on how we use these surveillance technologies? Are we so obsessed with safety that we must be suspicious of people we live with? As a conceptual design project, Happylife shows the scenario in which we mistrust in our ability to accurately judge other people, even those we are close with. Instead, we let machines make decisions about human character and human experiences.

Going back to the use of the word “noninvasive,” Auger and Loizeau pose the question of how much information we are willing to make available. With advances in technology, anyone can generate a large volume of information just through their electronics. How is this large, complex dataset processed? We defer to the higher powers of algorithms and artificial intelligence to see patterns too complicated for people. Even though we demand control over our lives, we surrender control to our devices with ambiguous levels of willingness and awareness.

What happens when we take AI into our homes and let it manipulate our physical and emotional experiences? We have the beginnings of an answer. Currently, our living environments are getting more personalized through the rise of smart homes and the internet of things, referring to embedding electronics into physical objects and creating networks among these objects. Products are designed to respond to our commands, automate labor, and make our lives more comfortable. Should we go further, allowing technology more decision-making abilities to improve our lives?
